United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) World Heritage List contains sites which are designated as having “outstanding universal value” under the Convention Concerning the Protection of the World Cultural and Natural Heritage.

This document was adopted by UNESCO in 1972 and formally took effect in 1975 after having been ratified by 20 countries. It provides a framework for international cooperation in preserving and protecting cultural treasures and natural areas throughout the world.

Despite beginning in 1975, sites were continuously being added to the list. Europe has the largest number of sites, with a over 180 sites being recognised between 1990 and 2000.

Oceania did not have a site on the list until over 10 years after the list was created.

A unique part of the sites are their size, with some of the smallest sites being only a few square meters in size while the largest site 'French Austral Lands and Seas' which is located in Europe measures 67,297,900 hectares.

The bubble plot explores the average size of sites in their construction year across each region. A key highlight of this plot, is two of the largest average areas for a given year, were both from 2010.

Unfortunately, a number of the World Heritage sites are in danger. The 'List of World Heritage Sites' was compiled by UNESCO and is intended to increase awareness of the threats to these sites and to increase counteractive measures.

Despite only having a small number of sites, Oceania has the highest percentage of sites in danger, while Australia has the lowest, with no known danger sites.
